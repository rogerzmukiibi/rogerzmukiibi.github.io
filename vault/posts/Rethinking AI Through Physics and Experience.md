I recently watched two videos that stuck with me. I’ll admit, I had them playing in the background while doing chores, so I’ll revisit them more carefully, but even on a first pass, they sparked something worth writing about.

The first was from Welsh Labs on AI models: https://youtu.be/2hcsmtkSzIw

One key idea stood out: large language models may not be the final form of AI. Instead, the future likely belongs to systems that learn through interaction, similar to how AlphaGo trained itself through self-play rather than relying purely on human data. That shift from learning from us to learning independently is really important.

The second video was from Veritasium on the three-body problem: https://youtu.be/XKSjCOKDtpk

What struck me here was more personal. I found myself recognizing many of the ideas, even when they seemed abstract or counterintuitive. That’s something I genuinely appreciate about studying electrical engineering. The field exposes you to a wide spectrum of concepts, from classical systems to quantum behavior, so even the strangest physics rarely feels completely foreign. At some point, you’ve seen a version of it, whether in a lecture, a lab, or buried in a textbook.

To me, electrical engineering is one of the best degrees for a curious mind. It doesn’t just teach solutions, it builds intuition across domains.

Now, why connect these two videos?

Physics evolves as our understanding deepens. What we consider “truth” today is often just the best approximation we have so far. From Newtonian mechanics to quantum theory, each leap reshapes how we interpret reality. If intelligence, human or artificial, is trained only on existing knowledge, it inherits those limitations.

That raises an interesting question:  
What happens when AI is trained only on our current understanding of the world?

It becomes bounded by it.

An AI trained before Newton would lack the framework for classical mechanics. One trained before quantum theory would miss an entire layer of reality. So if we rely purely on static data, we risk freezing intelligence at a specific point in human progress.

This is why the idea of experience-based learning feels so powerful.

Instead of feeding AI only our interpretations of the world, we could allow it to interact with the world directly, learning patterns, testing hypotheses, and building its own models. Not just consuming knowledge, but generating it.

This perspective aligns with what’s described in _The Bitter Lesson_: http://www.incompleteideas.net/IncIdeas/BitterLesson.html

And further expanded in this paper:
https://storage.googleapis.com/deepmind-media/Era-of-Experience%20/The%20Era%20of%20Experience%20Paper.pdf

The core idea is simple but profound: systems that learn from raw experience and scale with computation tend to outperform those built on handcrafted human knowledge.

So maybe the future of AI isn’t just bigger models trained on more data.

Maybe it’s systems that explore, experiment, and evolve, just like we do.

And if that’s true, then the real question isn’t how much data we can give AI.

It’s what kind of experiences we allow it to have.
